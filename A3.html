Abdul kalam Sir

Avul Pakir Jainulabdeen Abdul Kalam, widely known as A.P.J. Abdul Kalam, was an Indian aerospace scientist and politician who served as the 11th President of India from 2002 to 2007. He was born on October 15, 1931, in Rameswaram, Tamil Nadu, and is affectionately remembered as the "People's President". Kalam's life and career are a testament to his brilliance, dedication, and unwavering commitment to India's progress. Kalam's early life was marked by humble beginnings. His family were involved in trading goods and transporting passengers between Rameswaram and the mainland. Despite facing financial constraints, Kalam excelled in his studies, particularly in mathematics and physics. He went on to graduate from Madras Institute of Technology with a degree in aerospace engineering. His professional journey began at the Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO), where he played a crucial role in developing India's first satellite launch vehicle. He later joined the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO) and spearheaded the Rohini satellite program, further solidifying India's space capabilities. Kalam's significant contributions to India's missile program, particularly the Agni and Prithvi missiles, earned him the moniker "Missile Man of India"

NATURAM GODDSEY

Nathuram Godse, born in 1910, is infamous for assassinating Mahatma Gandhi on January 30, 1948. A staunch Hindu nationalist, Godse believed Gandhi's policies favored Muslims and were detrimental to India's interests, particularly regarding the partition. His actions, while condemned by many, are still celebrated by some Hindu nationalist groups.

ATAL BIHARI VAJEPAYEE

Atal Bihari Vajpayee, born on December 25, 1924, was a prominent Indian politician and a key figure in the Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP). He served as India's Prime Minister three times, most notably from 1998 to 2004. Vajpayee was known for his eloquent oratory, his moderate stance on Hindutva, and his focus on economic development and foreign policy.
